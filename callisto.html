CALLISTO

By Survivors, For Survivors.

 

Callisto is a third-party college sexual assault reporting system directed under Sexual Health Innovations. With a team of three human centered designers, we collaborated with the Callisto team to redesign the visual presence of Callisto’s brand and improved the user interface & experience of the website, projectcallisto.org.

 

"Sexual assault is a pervasive problem on college campuses in the United States – an estimated 20% of women are sexually assaulted during their college career, as are many men. Only 13% of college rape survivors report their rape to local police, campus security, or other authorities, and only 2% of survivors who were assaulted while incapacitated report. It is estimated that repeat assailants commit 90% of these assaults and that if these assailants were stopped after their second assault, 60% of rapes would be prevented" (Callisto).

 

Callisto is a third-party college sexual assault recording and reporting system designed to provide a more empowering reporting experience for survivors and facilitate the identification of repeat assailants. The goal of this project is to provide a more empowering reporting experience for student sexual assault survivors, increase the official reporting and identification of repeat offender, improve the quality of evidence for use in sexual assault judicial proceedings, and connect sexual assault survivors with local and national support and resources.

 

The new design, however, has not been launched on the Callisto website yet, as we are still in the process of working with the Callisto team to finalize the details and logistics.

The Design Process

The User Research

Performing the user research was tricky for this project, as it revolves around an incredibly sensitive issue. Therefore, it was important for my team to establish a comfortable, appropriate, and anonymous research process. To our benefit, the Callisto team had already performed extensive research on the topic and the potential users of the website, which became a substantial foundation of our design process. The research also guided the direction of the color palette and the syntax/vocabulary used for the redesign.

 

Prototyping

Before we created the website mockup, our team designed low-fidelity prototypes to get an understanding of the potential format of the website. We utilized our research to develop wireframes of the website and performed preliminary user testing before we proceeded to create our medium-fidelity prototypes.

 

To ensure usability, we incorporated a fixed navigation bar and interactive options on the bottom right and top left of the web page, respectively. The separation between the two menus creates differentiation, allowing users to easily detect both the navigation and the donate/stay updated functions.

 

Furthermore, we incorporated a page dedicated to the value of Callisto with user testimonies to support the cause.

Branding

Graphic Elements

Through our first round of interviews, we found that it was important to use graphics and testimonies that were as universal and unbiased as possible to account for survivors in a potentially sensitive state. I designed a friendly university in order to appeal to our college student user demographic. This graphic was generated with Adobe Illustrator.

Color Palette

In order to preserve brand identity, our primary colors reflect those found in the Sexual Health Innovations brand. According to the research conducted by the Callisto team, the colors were based off of research conducted to determine the most neutral color that wouldn't trigger any strong emotions. Our team also decided to incorporate hues of clementine orange to balance the gender neutrality that was lacking from the excess in blue. The colors also play off each other nicely, as blue and orange are complementary colors.

User Experience

When designing the first iteration for the Team and Advisory Board page, we wanted to incorporated a different geometric shape to break up the repetitive rectangular design. However, user testing revealed that the circular shape was random and did contribute to the cohesion of the interface. Some users also suggested that it would be more humanizing for them to have photographs of the team members, instead being limited to just a name and education/work history. However, the majority argued that seeing faces of the staff could trigger discomfort and uneasiness when browsing through the website.

 

The following screens display the first and final iterations. The changes were made according to the qualitative feedback our team obtained through user tests.

Though the first iteration was not the completed format of The Problem page, it received effective feedback during the user testing.

 

Immediately, users noted that they did not prefer the abundance of text. The majority of users admitted that the heavy text made it less welcoming, as they felt it was a hassle to read through the information.

 

When asked what would be a more appealing and effective solutions, users suggested that an info-graphic would be a better approach to relay the information. The team agreed with the suggestion and took it into effect. With a more visual approach, the infographic allowed us to condense the information, while maintaining a clean and minimal design.

 

The final iteration displays a grid layout that resembles the theme of the website. The team also considered making this page more interactive to engage the audience directly. Users can hover over the graphics in the grid to read more about the statistics of sexual assault.

Final Product

Working alongside the Callisto team, my team and I were able to completely redesign the Callisto website. We performed user research to determine appropriate terminology, colors, and designs that ensured the safety and comfort of sexual assault survivors, the primary users for this platform.

 

Then, we generated low-fidelity prototypes and medium-fidelity prototypes to conduct multiple user tests that contributed to the improvement of the user experience.

 

In the end, we developed a polished website prototype that Callisto can utilize to effectively launch their website.
