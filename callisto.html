CALLISTO

By Survivors, For Survivors.

 

Callisto is a third-party college sexual assault reporting system directed under Sexual Health Innovations. With a team of three human centered designers, we collaborated with the Callisto team to redesign the visual presence of Callisto’s brand and improved the user interface & experience of the website, projectcallisto.org.

 

The new design, however, has not been launched on the Callisto website yet, as we are still in the process of working with the Callisto team to finalize the details and logistics.

Why Callisto?

"Sexual assault is a pervasive problem on college campuses in the United States – an estimated 20% of women are sexually assaulted during their college career, as are many men. Only 13% of college rape survivors report their rape to local police, campus security, or other authorities, and only 2% of survivors who were assaulted while incapacitated report. It is estimated that repeat assailants commit 90% of these assaults and that if these assailants were stopped after their second assault, 60% of rapes would be prevented" (Callisto).

 

Callisto is a third-party college sexual assault recording and reporting system designed to provide a more empowering reporting experience for survivors and facilitate the identification of repeat assailants. The goal of this project is to provide a more empowering reporting experience for student sexual assault survivors, increase the official reporting and identification of repeat offender, improve the quality of evidence for use in sexual assault judicial proceedings, and connect sexual assault survivors with local and national support and resources.

User Research

Performing the user research was tricky for this project, as it revolves around an incredibly sensitive issue. Therefore, it was important for us to establish a comfortable, appropriate, and anonymous research process. To our benefit, the Callisto team had already performed extensive research on the topic and the potential users of the website, which became a substantial foundation of our design process. The research also guided the direction of the color palette as well as the syntax/vocabulary used in the redesign.

Design

Before we created the website mockup, our team designed low-fidelity prototypes in order to get an understanding of the potential format of the website. We utilized our research to develop a wireframe of the website and performed simply user testing before we proceeded to create our medium-fidelity prototypes.

To ensure usability, we incorporated a fixed square navigation bar on the bottom right of the screen as well as the options “donate” and “stay updated” on the top left of the screen. The separation between the two creates emphasize on the menus, allowing users to easily detect both the navigation and the donate/stay updated options. We also incorporated a page dedicated to the reasons why Callisto is integral and user testimonies to help gain more support for this cause.

Through our first round of interviews, we found that it was important to use graphics and testimonies that were as universal and unbiased as possible to account for survivors in a potentially sensitive state. I designed a friendly university in order to appeal to our college student user demographic. This graphic was generated with Adobe Illustrator.

Branding

In order to preserve brand identity, our primary colors reflect those found in the Sexual Health Innovations brand. According to the research conducted by the Callisto team, the colors were based off of research conducted to determine the most neutral color that wouldn't trigger any strong emotions. Our team also decided to incorporate hues of  clementine orange to balance the gender neutrality that was lacking from the excess in blue. The colors also play off each other nicely, as blue and orange are complementary colors.

User Experience

The following screens display the first and final iterations. The changes were made according to the qualitative feedback our team obtained through user tests.

When designing the first iteration for the Team and Advisory Board page, we wanted to incorporated a different geometric shape to break up the repetitive rectangular design. However, user testing revealed that the circular shape was random and did not seem cohesive with the rest of the website’s design. Some users also suggested that it would be more humanizing for them to have photographs of the team members, instead being limited to just a name and education/work history. However, the majority argued that seeing photographs of the team members may discourage some users of the website from feeling comfortable.

Though the first iteration was not the completed format of The Problem page, it received effective feedback during the user testing. Immediately, users noted that they did not prefer the abundance of text. It was not encouraging to read because it seemed too dense. Users suggested that an info-graphic would be much more appealing. This also allowed us to condense the information, while maintaining a clean and minimal design. The final iteration displays a grid layout, staying consistent with the other pages, that is interactive. Users can hover over the graphics in the grid to read more about the statistics of sexual assault.

Final Product

Working alongside the Callisto team, we were able to completely redesign the Callisto website. We performed user research to determine appropriate vocabulary, colors, and designs that ensured the safety and comfort of sexual assault survivors. We generated low-fidelity prototypes and medium-fidelity prototypes to conduct multiple user tests that contributed to the improvement of the user experience. In the end, we developed a polished website prototype that Callisto can utilize to effectively launch their website.
